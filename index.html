Objection Handling
Common objections and how to flip them. Use the Acknowledge → Reframe → Confirm framework.
The Framework: Acknowledge → Reframe → Confirm
👂
1. Acknowledge
"I hear you — [reflect their concern]"
🔄
2. Reframe
"Here's a different way to look at it..."
✅
3. Confirm
"Does that change how you're thinking about it?"
"We're happy with what we have."
Totally fair — most of our customers said the same thing. What we usually find is the process works, but at a cost nobody has added up. How much time is your AR team spending on payment matching every month? Most finance leaders are surprised when they calculate it.
"We don't want to touch our ERP."
That's exactly our model — we don't touch the ERP. We connect to it. Your ERP stays as the system of record. We sit in the layer between your ERP and your customers, handling everything from payment delivery to reconciliation back into the system. No disruption.
"We use QuickBooks Payments — that handles it."
QB Payments works fine for simple cases — but we hear a lot about fund holds, limited B2B payment options, and lack of AR automation. Bill360 plugs directly into QuickBooks and handles everything QB Payments doesn't. Worth seeing the difference side by side.
"It's too expensive."
Let me show you what the math looks like. If we reduce your DSO by even 15–20 days on [X invoice volume], that's [Y dollars] in accelerated cash. Card fee savings compound on top. Most clients see the platform pay for itself within the first few months. Want me to run that for your numbers?
"We already have a gateway."
That actually works in our favor — Bill360 can work alongside or on top of most gateways. The gateway moves the transaction; we automate the entire AR cycle around it. Not a blocker at all — and the positioning stays clean.
"We're evaluating Billtrust / Versapay / Invoiced."
That's smart — you should. What I'd encourage you to pressure-test is whether the other options actually close the reconciliation loop back into your ERP, or if they're primarily payment portals. That's where most solutions fall short, and it's where the real operational drag lives.
"Our customers won't pay a surcharge."
That comes up a lot. In B2B, most commercial customers are indifferent to a small surcharge on card — especially when ACH is offered as a no-fee alternative. There's typically about a 40% drop in card volume when surcharging goes live, which is actually a win: you stop absorbing the fee entirely, and most customers don't push back.
"IT needs to be involved and they're backed up."
Makes sense. We work directly with IT teams on ERP integration — it's a well-defined process and doesn't require a heavy lift on their end. Would it help to have our technical team talk to yours directly? We can scope the integration effort in one conversation.
"We built something internally."
How's that going? Most teams that build internally end up with partial automation — they solve one piece but not the full cycle. We're purpose-built for this, and we maintain the platform as payment rails, surcharging regulations, and ERP versions evolve. That's a heavy ongoing cost to build and maintain internally.
"Bad timing / not ready right now."
Understood — what would need to change for timing to be right? Is it budget cycle, IT bandwidth, or something else? I'd rather understand the real blocker than follow up blindly. And implementation is lighter than most expect — the ROI tends to show up quickly once we're live.

Gateway & Payments Intel
The stuff AEs need to know without drowning in the details. Cliff notes, not the textbook.
The Map Analogy
Payment infrastructure is like a city road map. The ERP is the starting point. Bill360 is the destination. Sometimes there's a direct road (native integration). Sometimes there's a highway interchange in the middle (the gateway). AEs don't need to be traffic engineers — but they should know whether the deal has a direct route or involves a highway interchange before they start giving directions.
What a Payment Gateway Is (for non-technical buyers)
A payment gateway securely transmits payment data between a buyer, a seller's ERP/website, and the payment processor. Think of it like a toll booth — it checks credentials, approves the transaction, and passes it along. In B2B deals, gateways also handle tokenization (storing card data securely). Bill360 is the processor and AR platform on the receiving end. A gateway partner like SnapPay or Payfabric is the road that delivers the transaction to Bill360.
Level 2 / Level 3 Data — AE Cliff Notes
The Coffee Analogy
L2/L3 is like the loyalty discount on your coffee — it's already there, you just have to show your card. Most businesses never show the card. Bill360 shows it automatically.
When a business pays with a corporate purchasing card, Visa/MC offer lower interchange rates if the seller includes extra line-item detail (Level 2 and Level 3 data). Most companies don't submit it — and overpay on processing fees. Bill360 automates L2/L3 data submission.
When to bring it up: Prospect mentions high card processing costs, or their customers pay by corporate purchasing card.

MID Structure — Why It Matters (Franchise & Multi-Entity)
The Family Credit Score Analogy

What ACH Is — In Plain English

It is a reliable, low-cost method for moving large sums between businesses — and it is standard practice in wholesale, manufacturing, distribution, and professional services.

Credit Card Processing 101
What every AE needs to know about how card processing works — and why cards are a core part of the Bill360 revenue story.
Why This Matters for Sales
Credit card processing is a primary revenue driver for Bill360. When a client processes cards through our platform, we earn interchange revenue. Understanding how card processing works helps you position it confidently, explain the economics to clients, and recognize when L2/L3 optimization or surcharging creates a compelling ROI story. Push cards. Enable ACH. Let clients choose. Capture the volume either way.
How a Credit Card Transaction Works
When a customer pays an invoice by credit card, the payment flows through several parties before money lands in the seller's account. The buyer's card-issuing bank authorizes the charge. The card network (Visa, Mastercard) routes the transaction and sets the rules. A payment processor (Bill360 through Fiserv) handles the actual movement of funds and settlement. The merchant receives the net amount after fees are deducted. Each party takes a slice — and the total of those slices is called the interchange rate the merchant pays on every transaction.
The Tollway Analogy
Think of a credit card transaction like a package being shipped across the country. The card network is the highway system. The issuing bank is the origin depot that loads the funds. The payment processor is the final-mile driver that delivers them to the merchant's door. Each leg charges a fee. The total of all those fees is what the merchant pays as interchange.

Level 2 and Level 3 Data — The Cost Reduction Tool
Visa and Mastercard charge different interchange rates depending on how much data accompanies a transaction. A basic payment sends Level 1 data — just the amount. If the seller also submits additional information like a customer code, tax amount, and invoice number (Level 2), or full line-item detail including product codes and quantities (Level 3), the card networks reward that with lower interchange rates. Corporate and purchasing cards — which dominate B2B payments — qualify for significantly better rates when L2/L3 data is present. Most companies never submit this data, meaning they overpay on every corporate card transaction. Bill360 automates L2/L3 data submission so clients capture the lower rates without any manual effort.

ERP Education
What is an ERP, how do they differ, and what does Bill360 connect to? Understanding ERP context makes you a sharper seller and a more credible presence in every discovery call.
What Is an ERP?
ERP stands for Enterprise Resource Planning. At its core, an ERP is the central nervous system of a business — it is the software that connects and manages the key operational processes of a company in one integrated system. Finance and accounting, inventory, purchasing, order management, manufacturing, HR, and reporting all flow through the ERP. Think of it like the electrical panel in a building: every department plugs into it, and nothing works correctly without it. When a company talks about "their system," they almost always mean their ERP.

For Bill360, the ERP is the starting point and the ending point of every transaction. The invoice originates in the ERP. The payment reconciles back into the ERP. Bill360 sits in the middle — automating everything that happens between those two events without ever touching or replacing the ERP itself.
The Electrical Panel Analogy
An ERP is like the electrical panel in a building. Every department (finance, operations, inventory, HR) is a circuit that plugs into it. The panel does not do the work of any individual room — but without it, nothing in the building functions reliably. Bill360 is a smart appliance that plugs into the panel and does one very specific job extremely well: getting invoices paid and the money accounted for automatically.
Cloud vs. Hybrid vs. On-Premise — The Three Deployment Models
Cloud ERP (SaaS)
Hosted entirely by the vendor; accessed via browser
  • Vendor handles all updates, maintenance, and infrastructure
  • Lower upfront cost — subscription model
  • Accessible from anywhere; ideal for distributed teams
  • Faster implementation; standardized feature sets
  • Less customization flexibility vs. on-premise
Examples: NetSuite, Sage Intacct, Acumatica (cloud), Oracle Fusion Cloud, D365 Business Central (cloud), SAP S/4HANA Cloud
Hybrid ERP
Core on-premise; some modules hosted in the cloud
  • Gives companies flexibility to keep sensitive data on-premise
  • Cloud modules for CRM, HR, or reporting; core financials on-site
  • Often seen in larger enterprises migrating gradually to cloud
  • More complex integration requirements for AR platforms
  • Common in regulated industries (healthcare, government)
Examples: SAP S/4HANA (hybrid deployment), Oracle EBS moving toward Cloud, Microsoft D365 (mixed deployments), Sage 300 in transition
On-Premise ERP
Installed and run on the company's own servers
  • Company owns the license and manages their own infrastructure
  • Maximum customization — deeply tailored to business processes
  • Higher upfront cost; internal IT team required to maintain
  • Updates require planned implementation projects
  • Integration with AR platforms often requires middleware (SnapPay)
Examples: SAP ECC (legacy on-premise), Oracle EBS, Oracle JD Edwards EnterpriseOne, Microsoft D365 (on-prem), Sage 300 (on-prem)
